Chapter 01

Early Literacy Methods

Foundational approaches to teaching reading to young learners.

Method 01

Language Experience Approach (LEA)

LEA is a reading method where students create stories or sentences from their own experiences, and the teacher writes them down for reading practice. This approach honors the child's voice and makes reading personally meaningful.

Example: After a zoo visit, students describe what they saw and the teacher writes their sentences on chart paper for the class to read together.

youtu.be/taH4pzbNt6k
Method 02

Teaching Through Singing

A literacy strategy where songs and lyrics improve reading, pronunciation, vocabulary, and fluency. Since children already remember the tune and words, reading becomes easier and more enjoyable.

Example: The teacher teaches "Baby Shark" to kindergarten students by singing together. After memorizing, the teacher writes lyrics on chart paper and points to each word while singing. Students later identify familiar words, practice letter sounds, and read independently.

youtube.com/shorts/J0shIsMBDek
Method 03

Guided Reading

A small-group reading instruction method where the teacher supports students as they read texts at their learning level.

Step 01

Before Reading

The teacher prepares students by discussing the topic, pictures, or difficult words.

youtube.com/watch?v=HFRcwN2x6Gs
Step 02

During Reading

Students read while the teacher supports comprehension and pronunciation.

youtube.com/watch?v=HZdIHuNtIbc
Step 03

After Reading

The teacher checks comprehension through discussion, questions, and activities.

youtube.com/watch?v=hTXQPIYLzoo

My Reflection on Guided Reading

The three stages of guided reading demonstrate efficient ways to organize reading courses. Before reading, asking students to look at pictures and discuss the topic prompted them to consider what they already knew. During reading, guiding struggling kids with hints and relating new words to familiar songs kept them engaged. After reading, word-finding activities and syllable clapping helped students learn phonics and vocabulary.

Chapter 02

Comprehension & Reading Development

True reading requires thinking strategies, not just word decoding.

Close Reading

An intense focus on understanding ideas in a short text. The teacher models comprehension strategies repeatedly until students internalize them.

youtube.com/watch?v=iKIUovilf5Y
youtube.com/watch?v=CZwDGCMbsts
youtube.com/watch?v=zIZZEvPRR9A

Shared Reading

Focuses mostly on decoding words in the text. The teacher and students read together, modeling fluent reading.

youtube.com/watch?v=Y2UDee7STBo

Orton-Gillingham for Dyslexic Children

This one-on-one approach starts by assessing the child's ability to recognize letter names and sounds, following the sequence: Model → Shared → Guided → Independent. Concepts include CVC words ("not," "fan"), open vowels ("no"), and silent-e patterns ("note").

youtube.com/watch?v=zZgS8Ij1Ltg
youtube.com/watch?v=j2hazzQ5bSs

My Response on Reading Comprehension

Keene and Zimmermann's article highlights that true reading needs thinking techniques rather than just word decoding. Skilled readers use strategies like asking questions, making guesses, and relating text to prior knowledge. Most kids are good at decoding but poor at comprehension.

Based on my experience, both learner types benefit from reading aloud with expression. When I make my classroom interactive with discussion, fun read-alouds, and student participation, even inattentive students start to focus and engage.

Keene, E. O., & Zimmermann, S. (2013). Years later, comprehension strategies are still at work. The Reading Teacher, 66(8), 601–606.
Chapter 03

Vocabulary Development

Building a rich vocabulary is essential for reading comprehension and academic success.

Strategies for Learning Words

  • Use a dictionary or digital device
  • Use context clues from the sentence
  • Search for images or definitions online
  • Ask a friend or teacher
  • Use morphological analysis (word parts)
  • Look at pictures for clues
  • Use schema / background knowledge
  • Choose texts at the right difficulty level
  • Apply the Frayer Model
  • Use Reciprocal Teaching: clarify, summarize, question, predict

Morphological Analysis

Understanding word parts helps students decode meaning. For example:

Ast (star) → astronaut, aster, astronomer, astrology

Five Contexts for Reading Instruction

  1. Language Experience Approach — experience, write, read, phonics
  2. Teacher Reading — model decoding or comprehension
  3. Whole Group Instruction — model, shared, guided, independent
  4. Small Group Guided Reading — 4–8 students, before/during/after
  5. Reading Workshop — mini-lesson, self-selected reading, sharing

My Vocabulary Reading Response

Blachowicz and Fisher (2011) explain that the best way to help students learn words is to provide a word-rich environment with opportunities to use words meaningfully. Students learn better from repeated exposure, wordplay, discussion, and reading — not just definitions.

In class, we discussed how vocabulary can be taught even in low-resource schools. Setting small vocabulary goals per class — where one teacher creates a class word list used across subjects — is a practical and effective method.

Blachowicz, C. L., & Fisher, P. J. (2011). Best practices in vocabulary instruction revisited. Best practices in literacy instruction, 4, 224–249.
Chapter 04

Writing & Reading Workshop

Student-centered approaches that build independent, confident readers and writers.

Writing Workshop

A student-centered approach where students learn writing through regular practice, guidance, sharing, and revision.

  • Mini-Lesson (5–15 min): One skill or strategy
  • Independent Writing: Students write on their own topics
  • Conferencing: Individual or small-group feedback
  • Sharing: Builds confidence and peer learning
youtube.com/watch?v=FXTA_mUYPKU
youtube.com/watch?v=uI2LBpCwyOE

Reader's Workshop

A student-centered approach where students develop reading skills through independent reading, guidance, discussion, and reflection.

  • Mini-Lesson (5–15 min): Reading strategy focus
  • Independent Reading: Self-selected books
  • Conferencing: Discuss progress and comprehension
  • Sharing: Builds communication and critical thinking
youtube.com/watch?v=LPHVmIOcFbg
youtube.com/watch?v=gviJBpfWLGs

Bibliotherapy

Using book reading to bring up and solve social problems in the classroom. Students often cannot express problems directly but may identify with ideas in a story.

  • Talk to parents at drop-off or pick-up
  • Observe children in different settings
  • Ask other staff members

My Response to Workshop Videos

The videos highlighted that education is not just imparting knowledge but understanding students' emotions and experiences. Technology can enhance learning but also create inequality — a critical concern in developing countries where urban and rural students have very different experiences.

Most importantly, emotional support and motivation are key to learning. Students labelled as "weak" may simply lack support, confidence, or opportunity. As educators, we should focus on creating supportive environments rather than labelling difficulties.

Chapter 05

Critical Literacy

Questioning texts, examining power, and reading beyond the surface to promote equity.

What is Critical Literacy?

An approach that encourages students to think deeply about texts and examine issues of power, fairness, inequality, and perspective. Readers ask:

  • Whose voice is included or missing?
  • Is the situation fair to all characters?
  • How could the story change from another perspective?
vimeo.com/939840927 — Dr. Glenn DeVoogd on Critical Literacy

How Do We Come to a Conclusion?

  • Evidence from the text
  • Awareness of context (social, historical, cultural)
  • Considering multiple perspectives
  • Evaluating impact (who benefits/harmed)

Critical Media Studies

Billboards, TV, movies, and advertisements all influence people. Critical media studies helps students understand implicit values, develop alternative narratives, and take action for justice.

criticalmediaproject.org

My Response on Critical Literacy

McLaughlin and DeVoogd (2020) discuss critical expressionism — expanding reader response through creative responses like art, dramatization, and digital projects. Critical literacy pushes readers beyond the text to consider power relations, attitudes, and unseen prejudices.

Analysis of The Tortoise and the Hare:

  • Concealed values: Dedication, humility, and hard work over natural talent
  • Missing perspectives: Other animals' viewpoints; the rabbit's background
  • Action for justice: Apply lessons to real-life situations where people are underestimated
McLaughlin, M., & DeVoogd, G. (2020). Critical expressionism: Expanding reader response in critical literacy. The Reading Teacher, 73(5), 587–595.

Critical Analysis: Encanto (2021)

The movie shows a distinct lean towards perfection and productivity — strong powers are more appreciated, reflecting a societal ideology that worth equals productivity. Mirabel is left out for lacking powers, and Bruno is isolated for his misunderstood ability.

Alternative perspective: Each person is valuable regardless of capabilities. Mirabel brings emotional insight and healing. The movie urges acceptance of diversity in talents, personality, and feelings.

Bush, J. (Director). (2021). Encanto [Film]. Walt Disney Pictures.
Chapter 06

Dyslexia & Special Needs

Understanding learning differences and creating inclusive classrooms.

Understanding Dyslexia

Dyslexia is a specific, neurobiological learning issue — not a sign of intelligence or lack of effort. It affects the ability to recognize, decode, and spell words, and is linked to phonological processing.

  • Varies from person to person
  • Often hereditary; can co-occur with other conditions
  • The brain is not naturally wired for reading
  • With proper instruction, the brain can adapt (neuroplasticity)
  • Students often experience anxiety, frustration, and self-doubt

Screening & Assessment

The Multi-Tiered System of Support (MTSS) provides varying levels of assistance based on student needs:

  • Phonological awareness
  • Working memory
  • Rapid naming
  • Vocabulary, phonics, decoding
  • Reading fluency
ca-diverse-learning.org/programs/Literacy-dyslexia

Teaching Literacy for Special Needs Students

Adapts reading and writing methods with flexible strategies: visual aids, multisensory activities, repetition, and assistive tools. Activities include picture cards, tracing letters in sand, and word matching games.

readingrockets.org — Hooking Struggling Readers

My Reading Response on Dyslexia

After completing modules on dyslexia, the reading brain, and screening & assessment, I developed a much deeper understanding. One key insight: the human brain is not built for reading — we learn by collaborating different brain parts, and when certain parts don't function the same way, dyslexia can result. But the brain is flexible — with the right instruction, it can adapt.

My main concern is that in South Asia, children are often labelled as "slow" or "untalented" instead of being properly assessed. I believe it is my responsibility to educate my community so that children are not harmed or misunderstood.

UC|CSU Collaborative for Neuroscience, Diversity, and Learning. (n.d.). Literacy & dyslexia.
Chapter 07

Conflict Resolution & Social-Emotional Learning

Building empathy, resolving conflicts, and supporting students' emotional well-being.

Transformative SEL

An approach that develops social-emotional skills while promoting equity, identity, relationships, and social awareness.

Example: In a discussion about bullying, students learn empathy and communication skills while also discussing fairness and cultural differences.

casel.org — Fundamentals of SEL

Healing-Centered Engagement

Formulated by Shawn Ginwright, HCE shifts from "What is wrong with you?" to "What is good about you?"

  • Asset-based — focus on strengths
  • Cultural — value identity and community
  • Political — address systemic issues
  • Adult flourishing — support the whole person

Peer Mediation

Trained student mediators help peers resolve disagreements peacefully. Steps include: Introduction (ground rules), Listening (each side shares), Solutions (brainstorm together), and Closing (sign agreement).

wpdi.org — Whitaker Peace and Development Initiative
youtube.com/watch?v=6lzIqdf7-3Q — The 17 Camels Story

My Response on SEL & Healing-Centered Engagement

I believe that if an educational institution doesn't know how to support trauma, the teacher-student relationship becomes non-friendly. In Bangladesh, mental health is often neglected or treated as non-existent. As second parents or guardians, educators can make a great difference. The teacher-student relationship plays a vital role in creating a safe space where children can grow emotionally and academically.

Ginwright, S. (2018). The future of healing: Shifting from trauma informed care to healing centered engagement. Occasional Paper, 25(1), 25–32.

Chapter 09

Course Reflections

Looking back at my learning journey through this course.

Throughout this course, I have learned that literacy is not simply about decoding words — it is about making meaning, questioning perspectives, and empowering learners. The journey from early literacy methods like LEA and Guided Reading to more advanced concepts like Critical Literacy and AI in education has given me a comprehensive toolkit for my teaching practice.

One of the most important shifts in my thinking has been understanding the difference between decoding and comprehension. Many students can read words fluently but struggle to understand what they read. This has made me more intentional about teaching comprehension strategies explicitly.

Critical literacy has been particularly eye-opening. Learning to question texts — asking whose voice is missing, what values are being promoted, and how power operates in stories — has transformed how I approach reading with my students. I now see that literacy can be a tool for social justice and equity.

I also gained deep insights into dyslexia and special needs education. Understanding that dyslexia is neurobiological, not a reflection of intelligence, has changed how I view struggling readers. I am now better equipped to identify signs of dyslexia and provide appropriate support.

The conflict resolution and SEL components of the course have been equally valuable. I now recognize that students' emotional well-being is inseparable from their academic success. The Healing-Centered Engagement framework has given me a new lens for understanding and supporting students who have experienced trauma.

Finally, exploring AI in education has opened my eyes to both possibilities and responsibilities. As educators, we must harness technology thoughtfully while ensuring equity and critical awareness.

As I move forward in my career, I carry these lessons with me. My goal is to create classrooms where every child feels seen, supported, and empowered to become a confident reader and critical thinker. I am especially committed to bringing awareness about dyslexia and inclusive education to South Asian contexts, where these issues are still often misunderstood.
